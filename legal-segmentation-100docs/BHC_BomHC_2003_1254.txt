In this petition, the petitioner has challenged two notices issued under Section 148 of the IT Act, 1961 relating to the asst.	Fact
1996-97 and 1997-98.	Fact
The petitioner is engaged in the business of manufacture of various abrasives.	Fact
In the returns of the income filed for the respective assessment years, the petitioner had claimed relief under Sections 80-I and 80-IA of the IT Act, 1961 (hereinafter called as the said Act).	Fact
During the course of the assessment proceedings, the AO issued notices under Section 143(2) of the said Act calling upon the petitioner to give further particulars regarding the claim filed under Sections 80-I and 80-IA of the said Act.	Fact
The assessee from time to time furnished the particulars and in the note relating to bond plant (p. 65 of the petition), it was submitted that the assessee had started the bond plant in the year 1991-92 for the manufacture of various bonds which are used in the manufacture of grinding wheels.	Fact
It was stated that the bonds are major raw material for the manufacture of grinding wheels, because it is these bonds that hold the abrasive grains together in a grinding wheel.	Fact
It was also stated that before the assessee started its own bond plant, it used to import these bonds from Norton Company, USA.	Fact
It was stated that Section 80-IA of the said Act postulates that where products manufactured by the manufacturing unit are used for internal consumption, the profit for the purpose of 80-IA has to be calculated based on the market value of the products manufactured by the unit.	ArgumentPetitioner
It was stated that the market value of the various bonds has been calculated by the assessee as follows : In respect of bonds which are developed by Grindwell Norton Limited, we have been successful to export the same to Dubai Singapore and in respect of these bonds, we have considered the export price as the market value of the bonds.	ArgumentPetitioner
We enclose copies of export invoices giving the export prices of these bonds.	ArgumentPetitioner
Other bonds are not sold to any outsider in India as a matter of strategy, and because of stringent secrecy requirements.	Fact
The company, therefore, has considered the Norton Company price as the market value of these bonds.	Fact
We have obtained price list of Norton Company, a copy of which is enclosed.	Fact
If the bonds were to be imported into India by any person, including your assessee, this is the price which the said product would cost.	ArgumentPetitioner
Accordingly, the assessee submitted before the AO that it has rightly considered the import export price as the market value of the bonds manufactured in the bond plant.	ArgumentPetitioner
The AO being satisfied with the explanation given by the assessee, passed the assessment order granting relief under Section 80-IA of the said Act as claimed by the assessee.	RulingByLowerCourt

CIT, bearing Writ Petition Number 1705 of 2003/decided on 29th Nov., 2003 has held that the decision of the apex Court in GKNs case (supra) does not lay down any proposition of law that in no circumstances the writ petition against the issuance of notices under Section 148 of the said Act can be entertained. Now turning to the facts of the present case, from the reasons recorded for reopening of the assessment, it is clear that the sole reason for reopening of the assessment is the net profit at 17 per cent of the total costs adopted by the AO for the asst.	RatioOfTheDecision
yr, 2000-01.	None
Nowhere in the reasons recorded by the AO, it is stated that there is a failure on the part of the assessee to disclose material facts in the return filed by the assessee.	Fact
It is not in dispute that in the present case, reopening of the assessment is beyond the period of four years from the end of the relevant assessment year.	Fact
This Court in the case of IPCA Laboratories (supra) and Bhor Industries Limited (supra), has held that if one reads Expln.	PrecedentReliedUpon
2 to Section 147 including the proviso thereto, then it is clear that the cases where the Department reopens assessment within a period of four years, it can do so on the ground of income having escaped assessment even if there is no failure on the part of the assessee to disclose fully and truly all material facts.	PrecedentReliedUpon
However, in the cases of reopening after four years, the AO must have reason to believe that the income has escaped assessment by reason of failure on the part of the assessee to disclose fully and truly all material facts.	PrecedentReliedUpon
It is held that the Expln.	PrecedentReliedUpon
2 cannot be read without reading the proviso to Section 147 of the said Act.	PrecedentReliedUpon
Applying the ratio laid down in the aforesaid cases, it is clear that in the present case, in the absence of any failure on the part of the assessee to disclose fully and truly all material facts, the reopening of the assessment beyond the period of four years cannot be sustained.
